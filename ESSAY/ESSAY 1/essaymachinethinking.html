Takealot.com: Ethical Interaction Design and the User Experience in South African E-Commerce

Author: Bonolo Morojele

Introduction

Interaction on the web is a crucial part of viewer engagement, which is fundamentally mediated through User Interface (UI) and User Experience (UX) design. These elements shape how users navigate, engage with, and perceive digital platforms. Alongside this, comes the prioritization of ethical design. Ethical design matter in digital platforms because it ensures technology serves people, respecting their rights, protecting their wellbeing and promoting fairness.

Takealot, South Africa’s leading e-commerce platform, offers a robust case study in digital interaction design. This analysis will cover its interface, usability, and the underlying ethics shaping user engagement. Through the lens of interaction design principles and critical digital theory and South African digital culture, this essay will evaluate Takealot’s user flow, accessibility, and socio-technical implications

Contextual Background

Takealot.com launched in 2011, growing rapidly to become a leader in South African e-commerce. As of 2023, it supports over 2500 third party businesses and serves millions of users nationwide (Adignite, 2023). The platform played a major role in popularizing Black Friday sales locally and expanded its reach through mobile apps and township partnerships (Reuters, 2024). This growth positions Takealot as not only a commercial platform but also a key node in South Africa’s digital transformation.

Takealot plays a significant role in promoting digital inclusion, by supporting local businesses in commerce. They help bridge the gap between urban and rural areas by creating a platform for local entrepreneurs to reach a larger market audience. Overall, Takealot helps integrate township businesses into the digital world

UI and UX Design Analysis

Takealot’s layout is intuitive, adhering to Jakob’s Law, which states that users prefer sites that function like those they already know. The familiar grid structure, a homepage that features logical product categories, consistent branding, and a top navigation bar with drop down categories create a low friction browsing experience. The colour palette (Blue for trust, white for clarity) mirrors e-commerce conventions that support user trust and recognition. However, while the design is functional it lacks aesthetic innovation. The UI prioritizes efficiency over engagement, echoing critiques by Flanagan and Nissenbaum (2005) who argue that most digital interfaces privilege usability over social impact. The result is an experience optimized for consumption rather than community

The interaction of flow on Takealot is designed to be user-friendly, particularly through its search, car, and checkout processes. The search bar is central to Takealot’s user flow, functioning with auto suggest and filtering tools and Search Results. With auto suggestions as the user types, suggestions drop down instantly, helping refine queries. The filtering tool assist customers in filtering their needs by categories, such as price range, rating and brands. The search results display products in an organized grid with images, prices, and availability.

Adding items to cart is straightforward, with a cart icon that updates instantly when users and items providing a quick view on selected items. From there, users can manage their cart by adjusting quantities or removing items before proceeding to checkout. The checkout process is structured into clear step by step stages, firstly logging in or continuing as a guest, entering a delivery address, choosing a delivery method, selecting a payment method with a list of different one’s users can pick from and finally reviewing and packing the order. There is a visual progress indicator that keeps users informed throughout, ensuring transparency and ease of use.

Takealot effectively applies Don Norman’s principles (2002) design to enhance user experience. The platform demonstrates affordances through clearly defined buttons and icons, such as the “Add too Cart” or “Buy Now” options, which suggests their function through shape and colour, and text

Feedback is constantly given through visual cues like pop-ups or highlighted updates when an item is added to the cart or when a user completes a step-by-step checkout process. Visibility is maintained by ensuring essential functions such as search, filters, and cart access are always clearly displayed and accessible across different pages. Additionally, consistency is upheld through uniform layouts, recurring iconography, and standardized actions across the site, making navigation predictable and efficient for both new and returning users. And, from Normans perspective, the design promotes discoverability and provides appropriate feedback loops. The checkout flow is minimal and mobile optimized, aligning with the high rate of mobile browsing in South Africa.

In the South African context, a mobile-first user experience (UX) is vital due to the increase widespread reliance on smartphones as the primary means of accessing the internet. Many users in developing regions like South Africa may not have consistent access to desktop or laptop computers, making mobile devices their main connection to digital services. Designing mobile first approach ensures that platforms such as Takealot are accessible, fast loading, and user friendly on smaller screens, even with limited data or network speed. This approach not only improves usability for a broader audience but also promotes digital inclusivity by accommodating the technological realities of most of the population. Furthermore, this design strategy aligns with insights from Nunes and Lee’s (2020) study on humanitarian documentaries, which underscores the importance of accessibility and inclusive design in technologically uneven contexts. While their research focuses on immersive media, the core argument about designing digital experiences that are equitable and context-aware is directly applicable. A mobile-first approach serves as a form of digital justice, addressing infrastructural limitations and promoting broader participation in the digital economy— thereby helping bridge digital divides in developing nations like South Africa

Takealot demonstrates several strengths in its digital platform design that enhances the overall user experience, such as personalization, clear CTA’s and fast loading times. With personalization, users receive recommendations based on their browsing and purchasing history, making the shopping experience more relevant and engaging. Additionally, the site features clear calls to action (CTA’s) such as “Add to Cart,” Proceed to Check out,” and “Track Your Order,” which guides users through the platform effortlessly. The CTAs are consistently placed and visually distinct, improving usability. These design elements reflect principles of user-cantered design that, as Costanza-Chock (2020a) notes, can empower users and reduce navigational barriers, especially important in contexts where digital literacy levels are uneven.

Another key strength is the platform’s fast load times, which are essential in a context like South Africa where internet speeds and data costs can be limiting factors. As Robinson et al. (2015) argue, digital inequality is not only about access but also about the quality of that access. Platforms that optimize performance for users with slower connections help close this quality gap and make digital participation more inclusive.

Takealot also demonstrates several weaknesses in its digital platform design, such as information overload, accessibility gaps, rural/mobile data challenges. In other words, while Takealot provides a broad range of products, the platforms extensive list can lead to information overload, making navigation and decision making more difficult for users. Costanza-Chock (2020) emphasizes that platforms should design with simplicity in mind. Platforms that don’t prioritize clarity and simplicity risk alienating users, especially those that have limited experience or access to streamlined interfaces

Despite its strong usability features, Takealot may not be fully accessible to all users. For examples users with visual impairments may struggle to navigate the site without sufficient assistive technologies like screen readers. As Robinson et al. (2015) highlight, the digital divide is not just about access to technology but also about access to usable content, which requires platforms to adopt accessibility standards that cater to diverse needs.

Additionally, a significant portion of South Africa’s population resides in rural areas where mobile data and internet connectivity are less reliable. Despite fast loading times, users in remote areas with limited internet access or high data costs may find it difficult to browse and shop efficiently. As Costanza-Chock (2020) suggests, platforms must be mindful of affordability and mobile optimization to ensure they do not alienate users in less connected regions, reinforcing existing disparities.

Ethical Interaction Design

Takealot use of Data and personalized recommendations are key aspects of its platform, which tailors’ recommendations based on user browsing and purchasing behaviour. While this can make the shopping experience more convenient and relevant, it raises ethical concerns about manipulation. Zuboff (2019) warns that such systems often manipulate users’ behaviour, using their data to push products they may not have considered otherwise. This approach risks creating a more exploitative user experience under the guise of convenience.

In South Africa, Protection of personal Information Act (POPIA) sets clear guidelines for how user data should be handled and protected. It is important that Takealot adheres to these regulations, ensuring that the user data is securely stored, properly managed, and not misused. However, the platforms transparency regarding how user information is utilized remains crucial. As Cohen (2017) points out, many platforms fail to provide users with clear, accessible information about their data usage, raising questions about level of informed consent in these transactions

Takealot employs various persuasive design tactics like recommendation systems and urgency tactics for example saying, “Only x items left in stock” or “Sale ends in x hours”. While these can increase conversions and enhance the user experience, they can also be ethically questionable. Persuasive design has the potential to manipulate users by playing on their emotions or sense of urgency, nudging them into impulsive purchases. As Fogg (2003) explains, these techniques can exploit cognitive biases, pushing users toward decisions that may not be in their best interest. Takealot’s use of such tactics needs to be balanced carefully to ensure that it does not undermine user autonomy

Takealot’s platform, while efficient, may raise concerns regarding labour ethics. The platform’s reliance on gig economy workers for deliveries and warehouse labour can lead to exploitation in the form of low wages and lack of job security. Srnicek (2016) argues that these labour practices are problematic, especially when workers are treated as disposable rather than as integral parts of the service ecosystem

Takealot’s platform must prioritize accessibility to ensure all users, including those with disabilities, can fully participate in the e-commerce experience. Binns (2018) advocates for inclusive design that considers not just technical functionality but also social inclusion. This includes making the platform usable for users with visual or motor impairments and ensuring that language barriers do not prevent engagement. By addressing these issues, Takealot can improve its ethical standing while also expanding its user base

Reflection and Critical Engagement

The case of Takealot reveals several critical aspects of ethical UX in the South African context. In a country where inequalities persist, Takealot has the potential to either bridge or deepen these gaps. On one hand, Takealot’s personalized recommendations and user centric interface design can make shopping more accessible and convenient. However, the platform’s reliance on persuasive design tactics and data collection practice may rise question about manipulation and privacy violations. This case emphasizes the need for ethical UX design that not only consider the technological and aesthetic aspects of a platform but also the societal impacts. Takealot’s design choices must be evaluated with respect to cultural sensitivity, digital inclusion, and economic accessibility.

The balance between business goals and care-cantered design is a delicate one. Takealot’s business objectives must align with the ethical principle of not exploiting users. User experience design should aim for mutual benefit, where users are empowered to make decisions that suit their needs without being unduly influenced by commercial interests. This requires a holistic approach, where both business goals and ethical considerations are weighed carefully.

Analysing the case study of Takealot’s UX design challenges has deepened my awareness of the significance in ethical implications that come with digital platform design. As a future designer, this case emphasizes the need for me to be mindful of the societal impacts of my work, particularly in a context like South Africa, where digital inclusivity, data privacy, and ethical labour practices are paramount.

A developing designer should prioritize care-centred design and ensure that ethical considerations are at the core in a creative process. In doing so, there will be a significant contribution to more inclusive, responsible, and empathetic digital environments.

Conclusion

Ethical and inclusive UI/UX design is essential, especially in a context like South Africa, where digital access is uneven. Design decisions shape how people interact with technology and who gets left out.

Takealot’s strengths lies in its ability to personalise, clear CTAs, and fast performance. However, issues like information overload, accessibility gaps, and persuasive tactics raise ethical concerns. The platform also reflets broader challenges around data use, labour practices, and inclusion

Ultimately, ethical design goes beyond usability and aesthetics, it is a socio-political act. Designers have a responsibility to balance business goals with care for users, especially the most vulnerable. As future creatives, designers must demonstrate awareness, empathy, and a commitment to justice for various audiences.
