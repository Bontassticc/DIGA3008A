WEEK 10: BLOG POST

What is an Ethical Internet to Me

An ethical internet, to me, is one that centers equity, transparency, accessibility, and collective care. It’s a space where all users, regardless of geography, ability, race, or economic background, can participate fully and safely. It resists exploitation, prioritizes human rights, and enables meaningful digital citizenship.

Main principles

Accessibility for All

An ethical internet ensures universal access to affordable, reliable internet infrastructure. As Noble (2018) and Costanza-Chock (2020) argue, digital systems often replicate offline inequalities. We must challenge the “default settings” that exclude marginalized users. This means designing for disability, low-bandwidth users, and multilingual communities.

Data Justice and Consent

Ethical platforms respect user privacy and demand informed, meaningful consent. Scholars like Kate Crawford and Shoshana Zuboff critique the surveillance capitalism model, where data is extracted without real choice. A better system gives users control over how their data is collected, stored, and shared.

Decolonized Design

An ethical web resists technological colonialism—where tools built in the Global North dominate how the Global South accesses and engages online. Nemer (2022) and Ali (2016) emphasize the importance of inclusive, localized, and co-designed tech that meets the real needs of diverse communities.

Platform Accountability

Corporations must be accountable for the harm caused by their algorithms. Safiya Noble’s Algorithms of Oppression shows how search engines amplify racial and gendered biases. Ethical design requires auditable, explainable AI that is continuously evaluated for fairness.

Freedom from Harm

Harassment, misinformation, and hate speech erode trust and safety online. An ethical internet cultivates platforms with effective moderation, community-led governance, and restorative policies to prevent harm and heal from it.

An ethical internet is inclusive by design, just in its governance, transparent in its technology, and rooted in care for people over profit. It is not a fixed destination but a collective project—one that requires ongoing critique, activism, and reimagination.
