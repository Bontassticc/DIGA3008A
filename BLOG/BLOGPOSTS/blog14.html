WEEK 14: BLOG POST

Ethics and Justice

In my work as an artist and a programmer, I see justice and ethics as foundational. Creating digital art and interactive experiences means engaging with audiences, technologies, and social structures in ways that can either reinforce or challenge inequalities. As I continue to develop my practice, I aim to embed principles of equity, transparency, and care into every stage of my work.

Drawing from Sasha Costanza-Chock’s Design Justice (2020), I recognize the importance of centering the voices and needs of marginalized communities in both design and creative expression. Costanza-Chock argues that design is never neutral, it shapes the world in which we live, and so it carries an ethical responsibility to avoid replicating systemic injustices. For me, this means always questioning who my work serves, who might be excluded, and how I can use my skills to amplify underrepresented voices.

Technologically, I’m also aware of the critiques of algorithmic bias and digital colonialism, like those discussed by Ruha Benjamin in Race After Technology (2019). Benjamin’s work reminds me to interrogate the tools and code I use, recognizing that software is not objective, it encodes the biases of its creators. In my programming practice, this pushes me to be transparent about the data and methods I use, and to prioritize accessibility, inclusivity, and user empowerment.

  • Use open-source tools and share my code when possible, to promote collective knowledge-building and avoid contributing to the walled gardens of proprietary software.
  • Incorporate accessibility standards in my UI and UX design, such as ensuring contrast, readability, and navigability for screen readers, so my work doesn’t exclude users with disabilities.
  • Critically reflect on my artistic choices and how they might reproduce harmful narratives or stereotypes. For example, if I’m using images, I’ll ensure they’re culturally sensitive and not appropriating visual symbols in ways that erase their original meaning.
  • Engage with communities I represent in my art or design. Rather than assuming what others want or need, I aim to listen first, involve stakeholders in the design process, and remain open to feedback and revision.

From the readings we’ve explored this semester, like Janet Murray’s (from dapt) insights on immersion and the emotional power of digital storytelling, I also see how ethical considerations shape not just content but also the form of my work. Digital media is immersive, persuasive, and emotional. This power demands ethical care: not to manipulate, but to create experiences that are honest, transparent, and reflective of shared humanity.

In summary, for me, practicing justice and ethics in art and code means listening deeply, challenging power structures, and creating with empathy and respect. I believe that these values are not limitations on creativity, but essential foundations for truly transformative and meaningful work.

References

Benjamin, R. (2019). Race After Technology: Abolitionist Tools for the New Jim Code. Polity Press.

Costanza-Chock, S. (2020). Design Justice: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need. MIT Press.

Murray, J. H. (1997). Hamlet on the Holodeck: The Future of Narrative in Cyberspace. The Free Press.
