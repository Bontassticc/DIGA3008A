WEEK 11: BLOG POST

Critical Reflection: Making a Feminist Internet in Africa

In Making a Feminist Internet in Africa, Sekoetlane Magenya presents a powerful critique of how dominant internet structures perpetuate patriarchal, colonial, and capitalist power dynamics, arguing for the urgent need to reimagine digital spaces through African feminist lenses. As a digital arts student concerned with intersectional digital justice, I find Magenya’s insights particularly resonant, not just as a political call to action, but as a theoretical framework for rethinking who builds, accesses, and defines the internet in the Global South.

Magenya positions African feminisms as not merely additive to internet governance, but transformative, challenging the very foundation of digital structures rooted in Western technocapitalism. She reminds us that the internet is not a neutral space; it is a terrain shaped by histories of exclusion and present-day inequalities. This understanding aligns with my own critical framework influenced by postcolonial digital theory and design justice, where technology is seen as a product of social and institutional power rather than a liberating tool by default.

The article importantly highlights how African women’s voices and bodies are systematically erased, surveilled, or exploited online. For instance, Magenya points to issues like digital gender-based violence and the lack of meaningful representation of African feminist knowledge. This makes me reflect on how interface design, algorithmic biases, and data infrastructures are rarely questioned within mainstream digital art or media education, spaces I navigate daily. We are often taught to design for efficiency, aesthetics, or user experience, without interrogating whose experiences we privilege and whose realities we silence.

Moreover, Magenya critiques tokenistic inclusion and emphasizes the need for African feminists to actively shape the internet’s values, design principles, and governance. This challenges me to think beyond diversity checklists in digital projects and toward co-creation methodologies rooted in solidarity, care, and local knowledge. Her call for a feminist internet that is grounded in African realities encourages us to design not just with communities but from their lived epistemologies.

In conclusion, Magenya’s article extends beyond critique into praxis, it offers a framework for digital activism, design, and scholarship that is rooted in liberation rather than assimilation. It challenges digital creators and thinkers like myself to recognize our complicity in maintaining digital inequalities and to reimagine technologies from the margins. In my own work, this reflection pushes me to center African feminist thinking as both critique and creative strategy in reshaping what a just digital future might look like.

References

  • Magenya, S. (2020). Making a Feminist Internet in Africa: Why the internet needs African Feminists and Feminisms. GenderIT.org, 17 March. Available at: https://www.genderit.org/editorial/making-feminist-internet-africa-why-internet-needs-african-feminists-and-feminisms.
